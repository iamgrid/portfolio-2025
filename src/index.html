My Core Values
as a Developer

I. Centering Effective and Fun Stakeholder Interactions in the Planning Stage Using Wireframe-Based Iteration

I rely heavily on an evolving deck of wireframes drawn in Balsamiq when ideating on new features with stakeholders. I can produce these drawings very quickly, so I’m not at all miffed about altering or completely redoing them as many times as needed.

Coding work only begins on a feature after the final version of the wireframe deck received full stakeholder approval.

There is a specific kind of feedback I’ve heard often over the years, which goes something like this:

“Andy is noticeably passionate about his job and takes pride in working to an exceptionally high standard.

He is solutions-oriented and excels at systems thinking, not just at the level of software engineering, but also in relation to the broader financial, marketing, and organizational goals and challenges of the company.

He is an effective communicator and always shows up to meetings well prepared, bringing a well-thought-out set of unblocking questions and strong deliverables to the table.

All of the above makes Andy a delight to collaborate with.”

II. Centering Maintainability and Avoiding Technical Debt

I believe strongly that the gold standard for production-level, maintainable code is both

  1. code that is easily readable by humans so bugfixes and changes can be easily done months or years down the line by me or any other skilled developer, and
  2. code that is only as complicated as it needs to be while satisfying stakeholder requirements in terms of features and future extensibility.

To achieve this gold standard:

  1. I go out of my way to avoid committing show-offy, “code ninja” code that is hard to understand and reason about.
  2. I think very carefully about the architecture of what I’m creating, only introducing an abstraction when it is truly necessary and fit for purpose.
  3. I go out of my way to leave detailed and clear explanations (comments, documentation and even full walkthrough videos) in place when I’m doing something complicated or unusual in the codebase.
  4. I prioritize writing automated tests that mirror real-world usage to ensure reliability and maintainability.
  5. I have my AI tools under strict supervision at all times: I read every single line of code I commit and never let a tool make architecture-level decisions in my stead.

III. Centering the End User

The details I sweat the most are the ones that affect the end user’s experience (UX) in a direct way.

Having a UI that is clearly laid out and intuitive, with button labels that are as obvious as humanly possible, and forms that a user can navigate with just their keyboard, down to small-time stuff, like how dates and times are displayed isn’t just good UX. Having all these things just right (by holding the end user’s hand when needed while simultaneously getting out of their way) makes people’s day a tiny bit better. And striving to make people’s day better is a deeply meaningful and worthy way to spend one’s work life.

IV. Centering Mentorship

Another way I find meaning in my work life is by teaching others what I know. Almost every company I’ve worked for afforded me the privilege to provide long-term, 6+ month mentorship to some younger colleagues.

AI has become exceptionally good at teaching people about specific, narrow topics. But AI can only be effective for scholarship when the budding professionals utilizing it have the requisite foundational knowledge and routine to spot when a chatbot is hallucinating.

Just as importantly, AI will never be able to answer questions that weren’t asked, or keep a young developer’s months-long journey "in context" in order to spot and mend knowledge gaps and areas of particular struggle.

AI also can’t really teach professional conduct, or combat the wild swings between imposter syndrome and overconfidence that almost all beginner developers experience.

You will always need a flesh-and-blood senior armed with the right mindset to help with these fuzzy, deeply human aspects of our profession.

V. Centering Mental Health

Everything I described above takes time and a huge amount of mental effort on my part. I build things cost effectively and to a very high standard, but I’m not fast.

I know and accept that there are situations where “crunch time” is genuinely unavoidable, and I’m okay taking one for the team when needed. But I’m not open to risking my own health by working under uncompromising deadlines week in and week out.

I understand that a majority of managers in our industry prefer to (or are forced to) prioritize speed of delivery above all else—I’m obviously not the right fit for such an environment. But if you have the luxury to be different, to think long term, to care about UX and maintainable software then I’ll probably be one of your favorite teammates.
